FYDP in a Pandemic: Sourcing Fabrication Material January 13, 2021 - Fabrication Team

A crucial component to any engineering project is to have a reliable way to source material in a timely manner that aligns with the anticipated timeline of the project. Components that are overlooked in the design phase and must be sourced last minute, or specialty components that have longer lead times, can easier derail a projects timeline if they are forgotten about. This is especially true for a final year design project (FYDP) that only spans a 32-week period, of which less than 20 weeks exist between the completion of the design phase and the required demonstration of a prototypical version of the project.

Obviously, as with everything else in the past year, the COVID-19 pandemic has disturbed established supply chains and put greater strain on the common ways to source material the world over. Furthermore, as many supply chains are global and multifaceted in nature it is hard to foresee shortfalls in the production and distribution of components. For this reason, our group put a great emphasis on sourcing material as early as possible to avoid negatively impacting build and testing timelines in the second half of the FYDP that are time sensitive. All of the electronic components were ordered online by November 28th, 2020, to give ample time for components to be shipped before the new year. In all, the components came from Amazon, buyapi, and BC Robotics to supplement those that the team already had on hand, with the last component arriving on January 13, 2021.

In contrast, it was quicker and more economical to source many of the mechanical components of the WAKE system from the physical location directly. For instance, the wood stain would have cost twice as much if purchased from Amazon as buying it at a hardwood store. As well, there is the added benefit to see the quality of said components, which, when building so many pieces out of hardwood is an important consideration. Furthermore, purchasing hardwood is not as straightforward as plywood or standard cuts at a local hardware store, so it was necessary to locate a source of cherry hardwood that could be visited even in the middle of the current pandemic. For this purpose, our group visited AJM Specialty Lumber in Kitchener, a local retailer of such hardwood and of specialty woodworking services.

As one can imagine, hardwood, unlike plywood, does not come in standard precut sizes as trees grow in a range of shapes and diameters. To get the most out of each tree, cuts are ununiform and are made up of random lengths and diameters depending on the grade of the wood. The only dimension that is standardized being the thickness of the pieces (e.g. 1”, 1.25”, 2”). The industry uses a metric known as board feet or board measure, which is a 12” x 12” x 1” piece of hardwood as the standard sale unit. After viewing the pieces of cherry hardwood available and subtracting the defects in the wood like knots and stains, we purchased 5 board feet of ½” thick wood for the front frame of the mirror and 4.5 board feet of 1” thick cherry wood for the main enclosure of the mirror.

The remaining mechanical components were purchased from Lee Valley (LED lighting channel, camera cover, wood glue, metal brackets), Fastenal (for the calculated wood screws), the Engineering Machine Shop (for the French Cleat plywood and acrylic), and Rona (for the cherry wood stain). As well, the only electrical component that was purchased in person was the 4 NMOS transistors that were acquired from Sayal Electronics, as before it was cheaper than purchasing it from Amazon. All of these purchases were completed by December 8th as it seemed that a provincial lockdown was looming in the near future. The only exception being for the cherrywood stain that was purchased in the new year after consulting with Cindy Salmon about what would give the best appearance and finish to the product once fabrication was complete.
