FYDP in a Pandemic: Fabrication Location, Equipment, and Safety January 20, 2021 - Fabrication Team

The pandemic added a considerable variable when planning for the fabrication and assembly stages of the FYDP. Hence, multiple plans were initially considered to mitigate risks associated with various levels of lockdown. The initial plan was working in the garage at the apartment of one of the team members. The next plan was working on campus at Waterloo’s engineering student machine shop. Following this was working at family members contractor company in London, Ontario. The final option was using job requests through the engineering machine shop to have components created and to do the final assembly at one of our team members apartments.

As the month of December came to an end, it became clear that the best remaining option on the table was working at the contractor shop. Salmon Plumbing, Heating, and Air Conditioning is a business with a shop located in the western industrial end of London, Ontario. It is worth highlighting the amenities of the space and major pieces of equipment that many other teams may not have had access to during this pandemic. In the image below the two main working spaces are the metal fabrication shop used for HVAC ducts fabrication (on the left) and general machine area used for scrapping old water heaters, air conditioners, and furnaces or making unique parts that cannot be done by other tools onsite (on the right). The workspace was only used during the weekend in order to limit impact on daily business and to decrease risk for all involved of virus transmission. Which brings us to the major consideration of this build post, the topic of safety.

Each of the main tools and pieces of equipment will be detailed briefly in the next section, but some general safety considerations can be discussed first. To begin, the large number of tools described here directly reflects the emphasis on using the right tool for the right task as a form of substitution. For instance, six different saws could be used in cutting tasks for the project ranging from a hand saw, jig saw, circular saw, mitre saw, and table saw depending on the action required and the level of safety provided by the tool. Furthermore, fences were put in place for both the router table and table saw when hands would otherwise get too close to the rotating pieces. As well, in terms of administrative control, having multiple people working together ensured no one could be injured without others noticing and helped to make sure the procedure for any action was well defined and understood by everyone involved before energizing any piece of equipment. In addition working indoors in a large, well ventilated space setup for fabrication helped to avoid the risks posed by cold weather or fumes when working with glue and paint.

All working spaces were kept clean and organized in order to avoid trips, slips, or cutting hazards. For each piece of equipment, testing of safety features was done before using it in fabrication. In the case of the router table, it was determined the red emergency plug would not de-energize the router if it was already on and would only prevent the router from being turned on it if was removed. Hence, it was agreed that instead, any time fences were being adjusted on the tabletop or bits being changed/moved the table would first be unplugged to prevent it from being accidentally energized. Similar precautions were taken for each piece of equipment and communicated clearly to everyone present before any fabrication was done with it. Finally, personal PPE worn throughout the fabrication and assembly process included safety glasses, earplugs, safety boots, properly fitting clothing, and the removal of any chains, bracelets, or rings that could get caught in rotating machinery.
