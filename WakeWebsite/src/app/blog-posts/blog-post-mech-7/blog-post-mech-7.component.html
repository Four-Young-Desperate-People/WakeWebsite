What does it take to make a WAKE? Part 5: Covering up; filler, floral flourish, and first form – February 17, 2021 Fabrication Team

After the glue had finally dried, further work could continue with the frame. The largest task remaining was to install a camera cover for the WAKE’s webcam that could be easily opened in the morning or closed at night when users went to bed. For this, a decorative floral bed bolt cover would be used as the movable cover on the frame. As the brass wood screw that came with the bed bolt was quite short and was difficult to screw into the wood due to its large diameter, a nail was used to first make a small pilot hole as shown in Figure 2. With this hole in place, the top of the bed bolt cover could be screwed loosely into place to act as a pin joint on the unthreaded shank which allowed the rest of the cover to swing freely side to side.

Next, to have the cover rest in fixed positions when the WAKE system was being used or at night to cover the webcam 3 nails were added to the frame. As shown in Figure 3, these cover nails had to be ground down on the grinder so that they didn’t protrude out the back of the frame. The cover nails were placed at the bottom and side positions along the arc of the freely spinning bed bolt cover.

As the steel nails are magnetic, the bed bolt cover was modified with a strong magnetic held in silicone underneath the surface features as shown in Figure 4. Additionally, the remaining brass wood screw was ground down to its unthreaded shank length, then punched into place with a light coat of silicone to ensure it does not dig or scratch the front of the wood frame. The results of this can be seen in Figure 5, with the camera held in place, and the cover magnetically snapped to the right side position.

Another feature that can be noticed in Figure 5 is the gaps in the wood all the glued mitre joints of the frame. Hence, the final step in preparing the frame for staining was to use wood filler to cover these gaps. While wood filler can be purchased it is cheaper and easier to match the filler colour to that of the working piece by using the fine particles of sawdust from previous stages of fabrication. This sawdust had been collected at each of the previous routing and cutting stages in preparation for such a need. It can be seen in Figure 6, that a small pile of fine cherry sawdust is collected on the back of a piece of sandpaper, while slightly coarser dust is piled beside. This is after the extremely coarse pieces had already been filtered out. Also, the same wood glue that was used for the joints of the frame would be used to create the filler adhesive. Taking the small pile of fine sawdust and gradually adding glue to it, while stirring the mixture with a popsicle got the filler to a near cookie dough texture that could be rolled into a tube. This tube was then pressed into the gaps of the frame, Figure 7, and the excess was trimmed away with a putty knife. Days later, once dry, the filler was lightly sanded with fine sandpaper, while the rest of the frame was sanded with medium sandpaper so that it was ready to be stained.
