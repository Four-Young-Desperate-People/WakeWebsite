What does it take to make a WAKE? Part 7: Holes and slots galore – February 21, 2021 Fabrication Team

Continuing from the last post, further modifications were made to the mechanical structure of the WAKE mirror to better integrate the electronics. While the screw holes were drilled out for the nylon fasteners of the Raspberry Pi board, it was decided to also add a hole to improve the natural convection around the Pi’s CPU. This was in the hope that it would help to prevent the board from reaching to high of a temperature and the firmware from throttling the performance of the CPU down. Using a corded drill, pilot holes were made in the corners of gap to allow the jigsaw room to maneuver as shown in Figure 2. The end result was a sizeable hole that would help to more air over the main components of the Pi board that tend to get the warmest under normal operation. With this complete the Pi could be mounted on top of the nylon fasteners to the Pi mounting board.

Next up was to cut slots for the power and webcam cables, as these would both be blocked by the plywood components attached to the back of the mirror when hung on the wall. Figure 5 shows the marked up sections to be removed in order to cleanly fit these cables through.

Using the circular saw shown in Figure 5, each slot was cut out by fixing the saw at a predetermined height. This height was determined by using a set of calipers to determine the approximate diameter of the cables, then leaving enough extra room so that when the French cleat and bottom lip were screwed on they would not compress the cables excessively.

While the LED wiring would not be installed until the end of fabrication process, it was important to add holes to the enclosure in order to be able to feed the wires to Pi from the LED strips. While the LED light strips had 20 AWG wire provided with them, it was determined that the largest wire to be used would be an 18 AWG 5-wire cable, as each strip required 5 wires; 1 for power, and 1 for White, Red, Blue, and Green. With this in mind, and leaving space to avoid the wood screw holes for the bottom lip as well as the bottom monitor bracket, a spot was marked on both sides of the enclosure inline with the hole into the front frame, as shown in Figure 6. With this done, a cordless drill was used to drill out the holes on each side of the enclosure.

The final set of holes to be added were the USB charging slots along the side of the enclosure. As before, the positions were first marked up on the side of the enclosure as shown in Figure 7. Pilot drill holes were used to create a gap that the jigsaw could cut from. The majority of the wood was then removed by said jigsaw, and the edges were made smooth by use of reaming the cordless drill and a circular file along the side profiles. This was done until the USB ports fit into the slots. Unfortunately, in order to make them fit cleanly, enough material was removed to either push the USB port in or pull it out completely in regular use. Hence, as shown in Figure 8, the metal brackets previous used for the mounting boards were modified to help keep the USB ports in place and flush to the outer edge of the enclosure. Essentially, the brackets were cut into two similar pieces while being held by a vise. The rough edges were then deburred on the grinder, and the top slot of the bracket was narrowed by striking it with a hammer upon the heavy metal surface. With this done the USB ports would be held firmly in place once installed, 2 of which are shown in Figure 9 below.
